DevOps influences the entire application lifecycle starting with the planning phase.

Imagine you work for a software development company in the finance industry. Your organization is facing challenges with software delivery, experiencing delays and customer dissatisfaction. The development and operations teams are working in silos, with little collaboration and communication. As a result, there are frequent deployment failures and a lack of accountability. To address these issues, your organization decides to adopt a DevOps culture. By building mutual respect among teams, clearly defining roles and responsibilities, facilitating continuous learning and improvement, committing to optimization based on business and customer needs, and embracing safe experimentation, your organization aims to achieve faster, more reliable, and customer-centric software delivery.

Imagine you work for a software development company in the retail industry. Your company is planning to migrate an online store from an old app to a new app to stay competitive in the market. The development and operations teams are working in silos, with little collaboration and communication. As a result, there are frequent deployment failures and a lack of accountability. To address these issues, your organization decides to adopt a DevOps approach. By building mutual respect among teams, clearly defining roles and responsibilities, facilitating continuous learning and improvement, committing to optimization based on business and customer needs, and embracing safe experimentation, your organization aims to achieve faster, more reliable, and customer-centric software delivery. However, your organization is new to DevOps and you need to foster a DevOps culture in your teams, introduce DevOps practices, transition from Waterfall to Agile, and select an appropriate framework to plan your migration project.
